Start your journey into Additive Manufacturing (FDM) with clear, practical guidance. We cover the core concepts, help you choose materials, and provide tools to estimate your projects.

What is FDM Printing?

Fused Deposition Modeling (FDM) is the most common and accessible 3D printing technology. It works by heating and extruding thermoplastic filament, which is deposited layer by layer onto a build platform to construct a physical object from the ground up. The process is precise but requires careful calibration to ensure successful prints.

Understanding Slicer Software

Before your printer can move, you need a Slicer. This software (like Cura or PrusaSlicer) takes a digital 3D model (usually an STL file) and "slices" it into hundreds or thousands of layers. It generates the machine code (G-Code) that tells your printer exactly where, when, and how fast to move the extruder and the print bed. Learning your slicer is the first, most critical step.

The Importance of Bed Leveling

For a successful print, the distance between the nozzle and the print bed must be consistent across the entire bed. This process is called bed leveling. If the nozzle is too close, it scrapes; if it's too far, the plastic won't adhere—resulting in print failure. Always check your leveling before a major print!

Key Terminology

  • Layer Height: The thickness of each layer, measured in millimeters (e.g., 0.2mm). Thinner layers mean better detail but longer print times.
  • Infill: The internal structure of the model, usually a grid or honeycomb pattern, measured in percentage (e.g., 20%). Affects strength and material usage.
  • Retraction: The printer temporarily pulling the filament backward to prevent oozing when the nozzle moves between non-printing areas.

Essential Calibration: E-Steps

The E-Steps (Extruder Steps per Millimeter) tell your printer how much motor rotation is needed to push exactly 10mm of filament. If your E-Steps are incorrect, you will experience either under-extrusion (gaps in layers) or over-extrusion (blobs and excessive material). Calibrating your E-Steps is crucial for achieving accurate dimensions and strong layers.

Solving Adhesion Issues

The first layer must stick firmly to the bed. Common adhesion solutions include: cleaning the bed thoroughly with isopropyl alcohol; using a thin layer of PVA glue stick for standard filaments like PLA; or applying a specialty spray for materials that tend to warp, such as ABS. A warped first layer, or "lifting," is a common sign of poor adhesion or uneven heating.
